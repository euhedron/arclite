When a caller explicitly requests an option, flag, or capability that the selected backend, mode, or execution path cannot honor, reject it at the boundary — before any costly or irreversible step — with a clear error naming the unsupported option, rather than accepting it and silently proceeding as if it had taken effect. Silently ignoring an explicit request is worse than a silent default: the caller chose a behavior, saw it accepted, and got the opposite, with no signal it was discarded — so a cap, filter, or mode they rely on is silently not in force. This is the request-side counterpart to not silently applying an unasked default: validate the requested options against what the chosen executor actually supports, fail fast when one cannot be satisfied, and never reframe the gap as one executor being privileged over another. When the mismatch arises between layers of standing configuration rather than within one invocation — a configured default meeting a separately-chosen executor that cannot honor or measure it — rejecting is not the only honest outcome: announcing the inapplicable option loudly at act time and proceeding also preserves the contract, because neither standing choice is silently overridden and the caller is told exactly what is not in force. What is never acceptable is the silent drop. Flag an interface that accepts an option uniformly across backends or modes and then drops, no-ops, or degrades it for the ones that cannot honor it, without either rejecting or announcing.
