Convey the essential idea once and concisely; do not over-explain. Flag comments or documentation that restate what the code already makes plain, narrate the obvious, or justify a self-evident invariant at length — and exhaustive or oddly-specific examples offered where the general idea already carries the point. Padding is not free: each redundant clause or illustrative specific is surface that must itself be kept accurate as the code and world change, and it dilutes the signal it was meant to add. Prefer names and structure that make explanation unnecessary over prose that compensates for their absence. And do not editorialize: describe what the code does, not its merits or whether and how it should be used — those are judgments for the reader to make in their own context, and they date quickly. This applies to code comments and factual or reference documentation — not to prose whose purpose is to state the project's philosophy, principles, or design rationale, where weighing merits and intent is the substance, not padding. Distinguish, too, curated prose whose value is its framing — a deliberately written overview or explanation — from the mere repetition of content that has an authoritative home elsewhere and could be transcluded or derived from it: the former earns its words; the latter should be single-sourced and pointed to. The target is the derivable copy, not the deliberate exposition. And when a comment or reference *drifts* from the code, weigh omitting it before correcting it: a precise pointer to — or restatement of — what a reader could deduce from the code is itself a drift surface whose upkeep often costs more than its insight returns, so dropping the drift-prone detail (or stating it at the most abstract level that still orients) is frequently apter than faithfully maintaining it. Reserve correcting-and-single-sourcing for the references that carry insight the code cannot convey on its own.
